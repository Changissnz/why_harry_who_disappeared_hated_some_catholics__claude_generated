# Why Harry, Who Disappeared, Hated Some Catholics
A tale of realistic fiction on a man who left the West after some time in the United States. Generated by Claude Sonnet 4.6.
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Nobody could say exactly when Harry left the internet. One day his accounts were active — arguments mid-thread, responses half-finished — and then they were simply gone. Some people who followed him online said it was the government. Others, who knew him better, said Harry had finally kept a promise he'd been making to himself for years.

---

The argument that finished him off publicly began, as so many things did in 2026, with the Pope.

Pope Leo had made a statement about the Iran War. He called it unjust. He called on the faithful to reject it. He spoke in the measured, resonant cadences of someone who understood that moral authority, deployed at the right moment, could redirect history. Opponents of the war — and there were many, scattered across the internet like birds after a gunshot — seized on the statement immediately. *Even the Pope*, they said. *You can't argue with the Pope.*

Harry could, and did.

He had been following the war closely, as someone from a part of the world that had grown accustomed to watching Western powers treat distant countries like chessboards. He was not a man who held his tongue when he thought something was dishonest, and what he saw in the rush to cite Pope Leo struck him, immediately, as deeply dishonest.

*Where were these Catholics*, he typed, *when the bishops back home were blessing the soldiers?*

He had done his reading. He knew that within the Church's vast and sprawling body there were priests, deacons, and laypeople of considerable rank who had expressed support for the war, some enthusiastically so. The Church was not a monolith. It never had been. It was an enormous, ancient, contradictory institution that contained multitudes — the saintly and the corrupt, the humble and the imperial, the genuinely devout and the nakedly political — and Leo's statement, however sincere, did not erase any of that.

The man Harry argued with online presented himself as a faithful Catholic. He was American, from what Harry could gather. He was also, Harry noticed quickly, the kind of person who reached for religious authority the way a debater reaches for a rulebook — selectively, when it supported his position, and never otherwise.

Harry's messages became longer as the argument continued.

He wrote about the Church's history of abuse — institutional, documented, and defended for decades by the very hierarchy now being cited as a moral compass. He wrote about the acquisitive nature of a Church that had accumulated extraordinary wealth while speaking of poverty as virtue. He wrote about the missionary movement, about the centuries during which the faith had traveled alongside conquest, and the cross had followed the flag, or preceded it, or been indistinguishable from it. He was not speaking about all Catholics. He made that clear. Most of them, the ordinary ones going about their lives, were benign people doing what people do — finding community, finding meaning, raising their children according to values they believed in. He had known such people. He did not dislike them.

What he disliked were the ones who got political. The ones who used Christ's name as a rhetorical bludgeon while demonstrating, through their behavior, that they had spent very little time with the actual text. The ones who could quote the Pope in one breath and support the bombing of civilians in the next, and see no contradiction, because their faith had never really been about the content of the Gospels. It had been about belonging to something powerful.

*That*, Harry wrote, *is the Catholic I am talking about. And you know which one you are.*

The conversation ended there, more or less. His opponent called him bitter. Harry did not disagree.

---

What most people who saw that argument didn't know was how long Harry had been building to that point.

He had come to the United States in his early twenties, full of the ordinary ambitions of a young man arriving in a country that advertised itself as the freest on earth. He stayed for twenty-five years. He watched.

The legal system, he concluded after enough time, was not a system so much as a performance. It moved arbitrarily. It punished some people crushingly and released others without consequence, and the variable seemed less often to be what someone had done than who they were and who they knew. What troubled him most was the inversion at the heart of it — that freedom, as the country practiced it, seemed perpetually to bend in favor of the malicious. Criminals thrived. People who robbed their neighbors, people who ran domestic terror through their communities, seemed to find the machinery of liberty remarkably accommodating. The protections meant for the innocent had a way of becoming shelter for the predatory, and when anyone pointed this out they were told they didn't understand freedom. Harry understood freedom fine. What he didn't understand was why it so reliably produced this particular outcome.

He also had little patience for the prohibitionists — the ones who had decided that other people's choices were their business. He had watched them operate in politics, in legislation, in culture, and he found them as dangerous in their own way as the criminals, because they too used the system as a weapon, only with more respectability and better vocabulary.

What exhausted him most, though, was the fighting. The country had reduced the most elementary aspects of human life to contested ground. Things that citizens of dozens of other nations received without ceremony — the right to make decisions about your own body, the right to learn without financial ruin, the right to experiment with your own existence, the right to earn a living that didn't leave you desperate — had all, in the United States, become battlefields. Women fought for the right to govern their own bodies. Others fought for the right to know things, to try things, to simply survive with dignity. These were not radical demands. In much of the world they were baseline assumptions, so ordinary as to barely require discussion.

But in America they required movements, and protests, and decades of litigation, and in the fighting everyone eventually looked like an idiot — the fighters exhausted and radicalized, the opponents dug in, the observers numbed, and the actual human beings whose lives depended on the outcome buried somewhere underneath the noise of it all.

He had watched some Americans, once handed any measure of power, reach immediately for dirty tricks — procedural sabotage, manufactured outrage, bad faith dressed as principle. It was not unique to one side of the aisle. It was something the system seemed to cultivate, to reward even. Harry had seen enough of it to stop being surprised, which was its own kind of defeat.

---

And then there was the deeper machinery, the one that had taken him longer to name.

Google and the rest had done something that Harry thought about often in his final years in America and continued thinking about long after he left. They had industrialized the information war. They had discovered, and then aggressively monetized, the fact that confusion was more engaging than clarity, that outrage kept people on the page longer than understanding, that if you could keep a population arguing about whether basic things were true, they would never get around to asking who benefited from the argument.

Harry had watched this reach places it had no business reaching. People in genuinely remote communities — people with no structural connection to national politics, no particular reason to have opinions about things happening thousands of miles away — were suddenly drowning in it. The algorithm found them. The outrage found them. They were handed enemies they had never met and causes they had never chosen, and some of them were changed by it in ways that could not be undone. He had watched people he respected become unrecognizable. He had watched the most intimate spaces of American life — the dinner table, the family gathering, the small town — infected by conflicts that had been manufactured elsewhere and delivered like a product, because somebody was getting paid for the delivery.

He began to see this as a form of violence. Not metaphorical violence — actual violence, the kind that produced real casualties, that sent real people to real wars, that justified the destruction of real countries. The information war was upstream of the shooting war, and America had become very good at both.

That was when he left.

---

He settled somewhere quieter. For a while it helped. He still read. He still had opinions. He still, occasionally, could not help himself from going online and saying what he thought. The West by then was well into a trajectory that he felt could not be corrected without serious internal damage first. Its culture had been coming apart at the seams for years — pulled in one direction by a liberalism that had grown bureaucratic and self-defeating, pulled in another by a rightwing movement that was not really conservative in any traditional sense but was something harder and more exclusionary, something that wanted to draw a border around everything and call it virtue. The liberties of ordinary people were being squeezed from both ends. The bureaucracy made life difficult in one way. The reaction against it made life difficult in another. The people in the middle simply suffered, largely unrepresented, while the loudest voices on either extreme were rewarded with attention and money.

Harry watched all of this from a distance and said so, at length, to anyone who would read it.

---

Which brought him back, in the end, to the Catholic.

The Iran War was the latest iteration of something Harry had watched for decades — a Western military action dressed in the language of necessity, opposed or supported along lines that had more to do with tribal affiliation than with any honest accounting of the dead. When Pope Leo spoke against it, Harry did not doubt the man's sincerity. What he doubted was the sincerity of the people waving Leo's words like a flag, people who had been silent through the cheerleading, silent through the dehumanization, silent through the long ideological preparation for the war, and who had now found, in a papal statement, a way to feel righteous without having done anything righteous.

The Catholic he argued with was that kind of person. Harry could feel it in the cadence of the responses — the confidence, the selective memory, the complete absence of any curiosity about what Harry might actually know or have experienced. The man had grown up in a country that had spent two centuries telling him his perspective was the default, and it showed. He cited the Pope. He cited tradition. He did not once ask Harry where he was from, what he had seen, why he might have a different view of what Western moral authority looked like from the outside.

Harry told him anyway. He wrote about the Church as he had encountered it — not in a cathedral or a theology textbook, but in the living history of places that had been on the receiving end of its ambitions. He wrote about what it meant for an institution to call itself the shepherd of humanity while spending centuries as one of its more aggressive landlords. He distinguished, carefully, between the faith and the institution, between the people who lived it quietly and the people who wielded it loudly, between the Christ of the Gospels and the Christ who appeared, conveniently, on the side of whoever had the most to gain.

He was not gentle. He had stopped being gentle about this a long time ago.

*The good Catholics*, he wrote near the end, *are easy to find. They're the ones not talking about it.*

---

His accounts went dark on a Tuesday.

Those who suspected official intervention pointed to things Harry had written — the war, the politics, the pointed criticisms of Western power that had made him a minor but notable voice in certain corners of the internet. It was not impossible. Countries had done more for less.

But those who knew him best believed the simpler explanation. Harry had called it, years before, in a post that most people had forgotten. *One day*, he had written, *I'm going to say the last thing I have to say to these people, and then I'm going to close the door.*

He had never struck anyone who knew him as someone who made empty promises.

The Iran War continued. The Pope's statement was discussed for another news cycle and then displaced by something else. The man Harry had argued with almost certainly moved on to other arguments, other authorities to cite, other opponents to outlast.

Somewhere — nobody was entirely sure where — Harry was probably reading, or watching the sky, or having a meal, or doing any of the thousand ordinary things a person does when they have finally decided that the noise has nothing left to offer them.

He had said what he came to say. The door was closed.
